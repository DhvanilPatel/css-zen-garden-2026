Where Light Dances

Tales from the Arctic Circle

In the frozen reaches of Svalbard, where winter nights stretch for months, the Sami people speak of the guovssahas—the light you can hear. They say if you whistle at the aurora, it will sweep down and carry you away to the spirit world.

"The sky caught fire in green and violet, ribbons of light folding upon themselves like silk scarves thrown by invisible dancers. My grandmother stood beside me, her breath a small cloud, and whispered: 'The ancestors are walking tonight.'" — Elina Nordström, Letters from Tromsø

Scientists call them solar winds colliding with atmospheric gases. But stand beneath them once—feel that electric silence, that hum at the edge of hearing—and you'll understand why every northern culture wrapped them in mythology.
